An E. coli culture was scraped from an agar plate with a sterile loop and inoculated into 500 mL of growth medium. The strain was engineered to express GFP (green fluorescent protein) and a target peptide drug.

The inoculated medium was loaded into a custom 450 mL bioreactor using a peristaltic pump. The bioreactor is a short cylinder (450 mL internal volume) with inlet and outlet protuberances designed to accept silicone tubing (2 mm ID / 4 mm OD). The vessel was fabricated by stereolithography (SLA) on a Formlabs printer using Clear resin v4.1, post-processed with 60 minutes of UV curing at 60 °C.

A total of 399 mL of culture medium was loaded, leaving approximately 51 mL of headspace. Aerobic metabolism is required for GFP expression in E. coli; the limited headspace may have constrained oxygen availability and hence growth rate and protein yield. Elevated temperature above refrigeration accelerates GFP degradation and may reduce peptide stability.

Peristaltic pump inlet and outlet ports were sealed with sterile syringe filters (Corning, non-pyrogenic, sterile). The bioreactor was placed on the benchtop and manually agitated periodically. Cell growth was assessed at 48 hours post-inoculation; cultures were allowed to continue for several additional days.

Sensor configuration: SHT30 (bench ambient temperature and humidity) and BME688 #1 (bench ambient temperature, humidity, barometric pressure, and gas resistance). Both sensors were mounted on the bench adjacent to — but not directly on — the bioreactor. Measurements reflect bench-ambient conditions rather than the culture headspace. No exhaust-outlet sensor or spectral sensor was deployed in this study.
